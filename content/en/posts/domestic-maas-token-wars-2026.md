<!-- ============ MOD-06 Cinderella's Glass Slipper Effect ============ -->
<div class="bis-section">
<h2><span class="num">6</span>Theory: The "Cinderella's Glass Slipper Effect"</h2>

<p>At the end of 2025, the overseas API service provider OpenRouter proposed a theory:</p>

<div class="bis-punchline">
<h2>👠 Cinderella's Glass Slipper Effect</h2>
<p style="font-size:15.5px;line-height:1.7;margin:0">
<strong>Users who come in during the first month of an AI model's release have a higher retention rate than later users.</strong> When users experience a model that works well, they develop <strong>extremely high loyalty</strong>, just like Cinderella putting on the glass slipper, and <strong>deeply bind</strong> their business to that model.
</p>
</div>

<p><strong>Strategic implications</strong>:</p>
<ul>
  <li>First-mover advantage + early users = a long-term moat</li>
  <li>Explains why big players dare to "lure users with low initial prices"</li>
  <li>Explains why Coding models can raise prices without losing volume (users are already locked in)</li>
  <li>Explains why a certain video model became a de facto standard after achieving 95% penetration in the short-drama industry</li>
</ul>

<!-- ============ MOD-06-Extreme Extreme Case of the Glass Slipper ============ -->
<div class="bis-signal" id="mod-06-extreme">
<h3>👠 6.1 Extreme Case of the Glass Slipper — Model A's 15 Billion ARR Economics</h3>
<p>The Glass Slipper Effect reaches an <strong>extreme form</strong> with Model A — it is no longer about "high user stickiness" but about users having <strong>no alternative at all</strong>:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>95% of short-drama API calls are handled by Model A</strong> ("It's almost impossible for a short-drama practitioner not to be a Model A user")</li>
  <li>Competitor Kling's ARR of 3.4 billion = 0.22x that of Model A, the <strong>gap is already 4.4x</strong>, not 4.4%</li>
  <li>The marginal cost for short-drama companies to switch models is extremely high:
    <ul>
      <li>Prompt engineering / storyboarding / character consistency workflows have all been re-built around Model A's APIs</li>
      <li>The team's prompt knowledge assets are accumulated on Model A</li>
      <li>Downstream distribution channels (Hongguo, Dianzhong, Jiuzhou) also recognize the "texture" of content produced by Model A</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li>Therefore, <strong>for Model A it is not simply "price hikes without volume dropping"</strong>, but rather "price hikes that <strong>cannot</strong> be substituted"</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Comparison with Coding models</strong> (price hikes without volume drop):</p>

<ul>
  <li>Coding models have <strong>multiple leading players</strong> (Company X + Company Y + Startup Z), so users can switch</li>
  <li>Model A is <strong>almost a single dominant force</strong> in the video generation track, and users' <strong>switching costs</strong> far exceed those in Coding</li>
  <li>Conclusion: <strong>The moat for video models is deeper than for Coding</strong>, and the point of no return in a one-on-one war arrives sooner</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Implications for the competitor (Kling)</strong>:</p>

<ul>
  <li>❌ <strong>Cannot</strong> poach users via a price war (Model A's ARR 15 billion vs Kling's 3.4 billion, <strong>a price war = suicide</strong>)</li>
  <li>✅ <strong>Should</strong> target <strong>niche scenarios that Model A does not cover</strong>:
    <ul>
      <li>Ultra-long videos (continuous storylines over 5 minutes)</li>
      <li>3D / virtual humans / digital twins (industrial scenarios beyond short dramas)</li>
      <li>Overseas Chinese / Southeast Asian markets (Model A not yet fully deployed overseas)</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li>✅ <strong>Should</strong> pursue an <strong>open-source / on-premises deployment</strong> route (avoid head-on API competition with Model A)</li>
</ul>
